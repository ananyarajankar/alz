What is Alzheimer's Disease?

Alzheimer's is a progressive brain disorder that slowly destroys memory and thinking skills, eventually impairing the ability to carry out the simplest tasks. People with Alzheimer's experience changes in behavior and personality.

More than 6 million Americans, primarily those 65 and older, are estimated to have Alzheimer's disease - more than the population of a large American city.

Importantly, Alzheimer's is not a normal part of aging. It results from complex brain changes that begin years before symptoms appear, leading to the loss of brain cells and their connections.

While it's the most common cause of dementia in older adults, other diseases and conditions can also cause similar symptoms.

What Causes Alzheimer's?

Age-Related Changes

Brain shrinkage, inflammation, blood vessel damage, and cellular energy breakdown can harm neurons.

Genetic Factors

Both early-onset and late-onset Alzheimer's can be related to genetic differences. People with Down syndrome are at higher risk.

Health & Lifestyle

Factors like heart disease, stroke, high blood pressure, diabetes, and obesity may contribute to risk.

Signs and Symptoms

Cognitive Symptoms

  • Memory loss disrupting daily life
  • Difficulty finding words
  • Vision and spatial perception issues
  • Impaired reasoning and judgment
  • Trouble handling money and bills

Behavioral Changes

  • Slower completion of tasks
  • Repeating questions
  • Wandering and getting lost
  • Misplacing items
  • Mood and personality changes
  • Increased anxiety or aggression

Stages of Alzheimer's

Early Stage

Mild memory loss and cognitive difficulties. Often diagnosed at this stage. Symptoms appear gradual.

Middle Stage

Increased brain damage affecting language, reasoning, and sensory processing. More confusion and difficulty recognizing family.

Late Stage

Complete dependence on others, inability to communicate, and potential bed-confinement as body functions decline.
